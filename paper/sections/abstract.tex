Biology grows robust computers from a single cell, without a blueprint or an external controller, and repairs them when damaged. We ask whether a local rule can be \emph{learned} to do the same for computation. A single per-cell update---a small multilayer perceptron that perceives four fixed features (identity, two spatial derivatives, and a Laplacian) per channel and updates its state residually inside a $\tanh$---is trained by backpropagation through a developmental rollout to grow small digital circuits on a cellular automaton. The paradigm climbs a ladder: an XOR gate, a one-bit full adder, and a two-bit ripple-carry adder in which an internal carry is produced then consumed. The grown circuits self-repair after a patch of cells is destroyed; and a single rule transports a bit correctly as its ports are moved and the grid is resized (position-invariant routing), though multi-input computation under such moves remains only partial. Finally, quantised to integer fixed-point, the learned rule executes as an ordinary program on a real Z80 instruction set, and the same datapath carries a forward-mode gradient. We scope each claim precisely: distinct capabilities are today distinct trained rules, most circuits beyond the gate are single-seed, position-invariant \emph{computation} is only partial, and the in-substrate gradient is a single-cell, single-step tangent verified against finite differences---not the training gradient---run on a conformance-tested emulator rather than physical silicon.
