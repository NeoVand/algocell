\section{Introduction}

Biology grows robust computers from single cells with no global blueprint. A fertilised egg develops into a brain; a wounded flatworm regenerates precisely the anatomy it lost. Each cell acts only on local information---chemical, mechanical, bioelectric---yet the collective reliably reaches a correct large-scale target and restores it after damage. Turing showed that two locally reacting and diffusing species can break symmetry into stable spatial form \citep{turing1952chemical}. Levin and colleagues reframe morphogenesis as goal-directed collective computation, in which cell groups pursue anatomical set-points rather than execute a fixed genomic program \citep{levin2019computational,pezzulo2016topdown}; in planaria, briefly perturbing the endogenous bioelectric gradient permanently rewrites the target morphology, evidence that the anatomical goal is held in a rewritable pattern memory \citep{durant2017longterm}. The lesson is that robust computation and its repair can emerge from a local rule with no central controller. This motivates our question: can we \emph{learn} such a rule for computation itself---not to grow a shape, but to grow a machine that computes?

Neural cellular automata (NCA) make the question tractable. \citet{mordvintsev2020growing} train a per-cell neural update rule by backpropagation through a developmental rollout, growing and regenerating a target image from a single seed---a differentiable model of morphogenesis. Follow-ups grow stationary textures \citep{niklasson2021textures}, replace the perception--MLP rule with localised self-attention for perceptual denoising \citep{tesfaldet2022attention}, and probe the robustness and controllability of NCA collectives \citep{randazzo2021adversarial}. The nearest CA-that-computes is the self-classifying automaton, which reaches distributed consensus on the class of a hand-drawn digit through purely local communication \citep{randazzo2020selfclassifying}. These systems, however, grow or classify \emph{patterns}: their target is an image, a texture statistic, or a class label reached by consensus over a static input, not a truth table read at designated output cells, and none is realised as an executable program.

Two further lines bracket the problem but leave it open. Classical cellular automata and artificial-life systems establish that local rules can compute universally, yet their rules are hand-designed or evolved, never gradient-trained toward a target function: von Neumann's self-reproducing universal constructor \citep{vonneumann1966automata}, Conway's Game of Life \citep{gardner1970life}, Wolfram's behavioural taxonomy \citep{wolfram2002nks} and the proven universality of Rule~110 \citep{cook2004rule110}, and Langton's self-reproducing loops \citep{langton1984selfreproduction} and edge-of-chaos analysis \citep{langton1990edgeofchaos}. Developmental encodings such as CPPNs and HyperNEAT are likewise evolved, and map global coordinates to a phenotype rather than emerging from local cell-to-cell interaction \citep{stanley2007cppn,stanley2009hyperneat}. Conversely, differentiable machines learn algorithms by gradient descent but are not developmental: the Neural GPU learns and length-generalises binary arithmetic as a monolithic differentiable machine \citep{kaiser2016neuralgpu}, memory-augmented controllers infer algorithms from input--output examples \citep{graves2014ntm,graves2016dnc}, and deep-thinking recurrent networks extrapolate to harder instances by running more iterations \citep{schwarzschild2021easytohard}. The closest neighbour, Differentiable Logic CA, learns a local rule that discretises exactly at inference, but it grows patterns and rediscovers CA rules \citep{miotti2025difflogicca}. None of these systems grows from a seed, self-repairs after physical damage, places its inputs and outputs with position invariance, and executes as a program in a concrete instruction-set architecture (ISA).

We close that gap. We train a single local rule---a per-cell multilayer perceptron (MLP) that perceives four fixed features per channel (identity, the spatial derivatives $\partial x$ and $\partial y$, and the Laplacian $\nabla^2$), maps them through one hidden ReLU layer, and updates its state residually inside a $\tanh$ nonlinearity---by backpropagation through time \citep{werbos1990bptt} across a developmental rollout (\ref{fig:hero}, \ref{fig:method}). Our contributions form a ladder. First, a learned rule grows a functional XOR gate whose output settles at a cell five steps away from its inputs (\ref{fig:compose}). Second, the paradigm composes into arithmetic, culminating in a 2-bit ripple-carry adder whose \emph{produced-then-consumed} internal carry a causal lesion isolates---zeroing the internal carry region degrades exactly the carry-dependent outputs while leaving the carry-independent sum bit intact (\ref{fig:compose}). Third, grown circuits self-repair after a patch of cells is zeroed and remain correct under asynchronous update (\ref{fig:robust}). Fourth, one rule routes a bit correctly across placements and unseen grid sizes it was never trained on, and computes across them only partially---a movable XOR reaches $\sim$68\% at $17\times17$ (\ref{fig:invariance}). Fifth, and the reason this substrate matters, the rule quantised to integer fixed-point executes as an ordinary program on a real Z80 ISA, computing the correct truth table bit-exactly, while the same integer datapath carries a matching forward-mode gradient (\ref{fig:substrate}). Unlike gradients trained into noisy analog device physics \citep{wright2022deep}, this one runs on a deterministic digital ISA: a learned rule and its gradient co-resident in one real instruction set, a bridge no prior cellular-automaton work provides.

We state the scope of these claims precisely, because honesty about limits is what makes the core defensible. The five capabilities are today realised by \emph{separate} trained rules, not one rule that does everything; ``one rule grows a whole computer'' is a goal, not yet a result. The largest computation is a 2-bit adder. Most headline results are single trained seeds; only the gate (8/8 seeds), the 1-bit adder (6/8), and the movable routing wire (9/10) carry multi-seed statistics, and position-invariant \emph{computation} remains partial---a movable XOR reaches only $\sim$68\% accuracy at a $17\times17$ grid. The substrate bridge is an offline provenance proof rather than physical silicon: each cell update runs on a third-party Z80 emulator (\texttt{z80-emulator}~v2.3.0) in a single-lane grid-to-lane layout, with the grid sweep and rollout orchestrated in software. Crucially, the in-substrate gradient is a single-cell, single-step forward-mode tangent---a Jacobian--vector product for a handful of weights that matches finite differences to $4.3\times10^{-3}$ at Q8.8 fixed-point precision \citep{wengert1964ad,griewank2008evaluating}---and not the reverse-mode BPTT gradient $\partial L/\partial\theta$ that trains the rule. What is new is the combination those limits leave standing: a gradient-learned local rule that developmentally grows self-repairing, position-invariant digital circuits and, once quantised, runs bit-exactly as real machine code carrying its own forward-mode tangent.
