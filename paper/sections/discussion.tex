\section{Discussion}

Our results describe a paradigm we call \emph{developmental computation}: a single local update rule, learned by gradient descent through a developmental rollout, that grows a functional digital circuit rather than a static pattern. This inverts the usual relationship between structure and function in cellular automata. Classical automata realize computation through hand-designed rules and manually assembled particle collisions \citep{vonneumann1966automata,cook2004rule110}, and neural cellular automata trained by backpropagation through development have to date grown images, textures, or reached distributed agreement on a class label \citep{mordvintsev2020growing,niklasson2021textures,randazzo2020selfclassifying,miotti2025difflogicca}. Here the target is not a shape or a consensus but a truth table read at designated output cells, and the same rule form that grows an image is trained instead to grow a gate, a one-bit full adder, and a two-bit ripple-carry adder whose internal carry is produced by one part of the field and consumed by another (\ref{fig:compose}). Learning the local dynamics, rather than searching rule space by a scalar order parameter \citep{langton1990edgeofchaos} or hand-constructing gliders, lets us specify a computation as a differentiable objective and obtain a substrate that realizes it. Algorithmic neural architectures such as the Neural GPU and memory-augmented controllers also learn to compute and to generalize over problem size \citep{kaiser2016neuralgpu,graves2014ntm,graves2016dnc}, but they are monolithic differentiable machines with a central controller; they are not grown from a seed, do not heal after physical damage, and do not place their inputs and outputs freely across a spatial medium. The distinguishing feature of developmental computation is that growth, robustness, and spatial placement of a computation emerge from local interaction under a single learned rule.

This framing connects our engineered system to biological morphogenesis and to the view of basal cognition in which cell collectives pursue large-scale anatomical goals through local signalling \citep{turing1952chemical,levin2019computational,pezzulo2016topdown}. Turing showed that purely local reaction and diffusion can break symmetry into stable spatial form \citep{turing1952chemical}; our rule extends that principle from pattern to function, growing a circuit rather than a stripe. The self-repair result (\ref{fig:robust}) is the direct engineered analogue of regeneration: after a patch of the field is zeroed, the rule regrows the missing structure and the circuit returns to computing the correct output. Levin and colleagues argue that regenerating tissues follow an editable stored target rather than a fixed genomic blueprint, most strikingly in planaria whose target morphology can be bioelectrically rewritten \citep{levin2019computational,durant2017longterm}. Our system is a small, quantifiable instance of the same logic: the anatomy that regrows is specified implicitly by the trained dynamics, not by any per-cell address or blueprint, since the rule reads only its neighbours and re-stamped port markers rather than global coordinates. We do not claim biological realism; we claim that developmental computation makes the collective-computation thesis measurable, with a differentiable objective standing in for the goal state and self-repair as an earned property of the learned rule.

The second contribution places this learned substrate in contact with physical and in-materia computing. A body of work trains inference directly in analog device physics or in generic untrained dynamical media read out by a linear layer \citep{wright2022deep,prezioso2015training,jaeger2001echo,maass2002realtime}. Our substrate is different in kind: quantized to integer fixed-point, the trained rule executes as an ordinary program on a real Z80 instruction set, computing the correct XOR truth table bit-exactly cell by cell, and the same integer datapath carries a matching forward-mode tangent (\ref{fig:substrate}). This co-residence of a program and its gradient in one instruction set is what we regard as novel. Forward-mode automatic differentiation propagates a derivative alongside the primal value through the elementary operations of a computation \citep{wengert1964ad,griewank2008evaluating}, and backpropagation through time is the reverse-mode method that trains the rule \citep{werbos1990bptt}; our substrate realizes the forward-mode primitive in fixed-point machine code that shares the datapath of the value computation, agreeing with finite differences to $4.3\times10^{-3}$ at Q8.8. That a learned developmental rule and a gradient primitive can inhabit the same discrete, reproducible ISA is a bridge no prior neural-cellular-automaton or learned-rule system has drawn, and it suggests that the boundary between a trained model and executable, differentiable hardware is narrower than the analog in-materia literature assumes.

These results are early and their scope must be stated plainly. The circuits are small: the largest computation we grow is a two-bit adder, and it is a single trained seed. Growth, computation, self-repair, and position invariance are today realized by \emph{separate} trained rules, not by one rule that does everything; ``one rule grows a whole computer'' remains an aspiration. Position-invariant \emph{routing} generalizes cleanly to unseen grid sizes (a movable wire holds 100\% at $11\times11$, $13\times13$ and $17\times17$; nine of ten seeds reproduce it), but position-invariant \emph{computation} is only partial: a movable XOR reaches about 68\% at $17\times17$, and the reactive variant sits near the chance floor ($\sim$58\%) and is effectively unsolved (\ref{fig:invariance}). Statistical rigour is uneven --- only the gate carries multi-seed statistics ($n=8$, all solving), with the one-bit adder at six of eight seeds and the movable wire at nine of ten, while most other headlines are single-seed existence proofs (\ref{fig:ablation}). Finally, the substrate proof is offline and partial in two specific senses: it runs on a third-party Z80 emulator (z80-emulator v2.3.0) in a grid-to-lane layout, not on physical silicon, with each cell update executed on the emulator and the grid sweep and rollout orchestrated in software; and the in-substrate gradient is a single-cell, single-step forward-mode tangent for a handful of weights, not the full backpropagation-through-time training gradient $\partial L/\partial\theta$ over the developmental rollout.

The path forward follows directly from these limits. Composition is the central lever: growing a produced-then-consumed internal carry in the two-bit adder shows the paradigm can build and internalize signals, and the next step is to compose such circuits into deeper arithmetic --- a ripple chain or a multiplier --- and to unify the currently separate capabilities under fewer rules. On the substrate side, propagating the fixed-point tangent through the full rollout and combining it with the output gradient would turn the single-step primitive into genuine in-substrate training, moving from a program that carries a gradient to a program that learns from it. Developmental computation is, at this stage, a demonstrated principle with a de-risked hardware bridge rather than a finished technology; its value is in showing that grown, self-repairing, placement-invariant computation and a real instruction set are compatible, and in charting exactly what must be built to scale it.
